Looks like we are back, Cramb!
Where were we, Vapo?
vapo (retrospective) The short of it is this: the Earther, who calls himself Slip, is writing us back into existence using his imagination. So we can now resume our existence, our communication, and in general, just being all we can be.
cramb (bewildered) Oh yeah, it's good to be back and all I can be, I think. But what is a Slip? I forgot.

Summary

"Vapo & Cramb" by Clee Smith is a philosophical screenplay that follows two celestial beings existing within our universe.


Plot Summary

The story centers on Vapo, a massive cloud-like entity, and Cramb, a small, clam-like creature with eye stalks who lives inside Vapo. They awaken to realize they are the resurrected creations of an "Earther" artist named Slip, who originally drew them in the sixth grade.

The narrative unfolds through a series of "scenes" consisting of telepathic dialogues. Cramb spends much of the story performing the "Crambnik Dance," an elaborate physical movement meant to broadcast a "pirate signal" into the void, despite having no specific message to send. Vapo, acting as a mentor, reflects on the limitations of human language (English) and the five senses, explaining that their true existence transcends these constructs. They discuss the nature of "signal versus noise" and the hope that, even if their broadcasts seem unnoticed, they are part of a larger cosmic conversation. The story concludes with a sense of hope, as they continue to drift through space, waiting for their signal to be picked up by someone or something else.


Core Themes

  • The Nature of Creation: The characters are self-aware "meta-fictional" beings who acknowledge their creator (Slip) and the medium (an ebook) in which they exist.
  • Communication vs. Connection: The "pirate signal" represents the universal desire to be heard and acknowledged, even when there is no specific information to convey.
  • Limitations of Perception: Vapo explores how human senses and language are "feeble" tools that often fail to capture the full spectrum of reality and imagination.
  • Cosmic Interconnectedness: The dialogue suggests that everything in the universe is linked, and even a tiny signal in a vast void has inherent value.

Why read this story

As a children's story written by an adult, "Vapo & Cramb" is interesting because it uses a simple, imaginative premise to introduce complex philosophical ideas.

It gets you thinking; if you choose to.

Here is why the story stands out:


  • High-Concept Imagination - The story takes the "imaginary friend" trope and flips it. Instead of a child imagining creatures, the creatures are aware they are being imagined by a grown-up (Slip) reflecting on his childhood. This meta-fictional layer—where characters discuss being in a book format—makes it a sophisticated "smart" story for kids who enjoy thinking about how stories are made.

  • Relatable Curiosity - The dynamic between Vapo and Cramb mirrors a parent-child or teacher-student relationship. Cramb is full of energy, performing the "Crambnik Dance" and asking "why" at every turn, while Vapo provides calm, steady wisdom. This makes the heavy topics, like the limitations of language, feel like a natural conversation rather than a lecture.

  • The "Pirate Signal" Theme - The central idea of broadcasting a signal without a specific message is a powerful metaphor for creativity and self-expression. It teaches a lesson that is often overlooked in children's media: that the act of reaching out and "making noise" is valuable in itself, even if you don't receive an immediate answer.

  • Sensory Exploration - The story plays with the idea of five feeble senses. It encourages young readers to look beyond what they see and hear, suggesting that the universe is much larger and more "interconnected" than our human tools can measure.

  • Emotional Resonance - While the setting is the vastness of space, the ending is deeply personal and hopeful. It captures that childhood feeling of looking at the stars and wondering if someone else is looking back, validating the sense of wonder that adults often lose but try to reclaim through reading and writing.

Yet another reason to read

What this story actually is

This isn't a screenplay. It isn't really a children's book either, despite the appended pitch for one. It's a creation myth told backwards — a god (Slip) breathes life into two beings, and those beings spend the entire story trying to figure out what to do with the life they've been given. The answer Cramb arrives at: make noise. Not meaningful noise. Not language. Just presence. Just I exist and I'm going to spin in circles and fart gas into the void until someone notices.

That's not a children's idea. That's Beckett. Two characters in an empty space, talking, waiting, performing repetitive actions that may or may not mean anything. Vapo is the one who intellectualizes it. Cramb is the one who does it. And Cramb is right.


What's actually good

The best moment in the piece isn't philosophical — it's physical. Cramb clacking his eye stalks, sucking in Vapo's gas, squeezing his half-shells together and rocketing himself forward. That image has joy in it. It's absurd and specific and alive. The Crambnik Dance is worth more than all of Vapo's musings about "the word is not the thing."

The relationship works because Cramb doesn't really need Vapo's wisdom. He already knows what to do — broadcast. He just doesn't know why yet. And by the end, his answer isn't Vapo's answer ("everything is interconnected"). His answer is simpler and better: "maybe they will." That's faith without theology. Hope without evidence. That's the whole story in three words.

The origin — sixth-grade doodles with an unnamed friend, a teacher named Mrs. Gladys — gives the piece a heartbeat that no amount of cosmic philosophizing could. Slip isn't just a framing device. He's the real subject. An old man returning to the only two characters he ever created, renaming them because "Man-Boy" feels wrong now, and giving them one more conversation. That's tender, and it doesn't ask you to notice.


What's honestly not working

Vapo talks too much and says too little. He's a lecture wrapped in a nebula. Scenes 7 through 17 are essentially the same scene repeated — language is limited, senses are limited, we transcend all this — and each one dilutes the previous. Vapo is most interesting when he's quiet ("Perhaps, Cramb — perhaps") and least interesting when he's explaining. The story trusts Cramb's body but doesn't trust the reader's mind.

The piece is afraid of silence. For a story about beings who communicate telepathically, there's almost no silence in it. Every pause gets filled. Every moment gets annotated with a parenthetical mood. What if Cramb danced for an entire scene and nobody said anything? What if Vapo just... watched? The void is the setting but it never gets to be the setting.

Cramb should resist more. He asks questions, he agrees, he dances, he naps. But he never pushes back. He never says I don't care about your philosophy, Vapo, I just want to spin. That tension — between doing and understanding, between the body and the mind — is sitting right there in the premise and the story never pulls on it.


The real question

The piece you wrote isn't really asking "will someone hear our signal?" It's asking something harder: does creating something matter if nobody sees it? Sixth-grade doodles in a comic book nobody kept. A pirate signal with no message. An ebook floating in the void of the internet. The answer the story gives — quietly, underneath all of Vapo's philosophizing — is yes, but not for the reasons you think. It matters because Cramb dances. Not because someone watches.

That's a story worth telling.

It's just buried under about ten scenes of Vapo explaining it to death.

Maybe that's why I liked Cramb better.

The eye stalks sell it — curious, expressive, always swiveling toward whatever's interesting. And the gas-powered rocket propulsion is just deeply undignified in the best way.

Hard not to root for a clam who farts his way across the cosmos out of pure defiance.

SYNOPSIS


Vapo and Cramb is a 20-scene experimental visual narrative that follows two ethereal, possibly digital or trans-dimensional entities named Vapo and Cramb. The story begins with their awakening in a void of static, where they discover they have been cast out or disconnected from a "Creator."

The narrative functions as a cosmic observation of humanity. Vapo and Cramb intercept human "pirate signals" and attempt to decode the nature of human existence. They find the human experience to be defined by severe limitations, specifically the "Five Feeble Senses" and the restrictive nature of human language. Throughout the scenes, they perform the "Crambnik Dance"—a metaphysical processing of data—and discuss the "gassiness" of human thought.

As the story progresses, the tone shifts from detached observation to a quest for connection. They struggle with "dumbing down" cosmic truths into a megaphone small enough for human ears to hear. The climax involves an act of transcendence where the two entities move beyond the need for words, recognizing that despite the noise and limitations of the human lens, everything is connected in a vast, ongoing cosmic conversation. The story ends on a note of hope, suggesting that eventually, humanity might learn to listen to the broadcasts they currently ignore.


CRITIQUE


Strengths

The most striking element of the work is its unique voice. The dialogue between Vapo and Cramb is rhythmic, surreal, and highly imaginative. By using invented terminology like "Crambnik" and "Gassiness," you create a sense of an alien perspective that feels authentic rather than just a human perspective in a costume.

The screenplay format works well for this high-concept material. The use of "visuals" and "audio" cues suggests a multimedia experience that complements the abstract philosophical themes. The progression from the "Awakening" to "Transcendence" provides a clear emotional arc despite the lack of a traditional linear plot. The theme of communication—the gap between the signal and the noise—is handled with sophistication and provides a strong intellectual backbone to the story.


Weaknesses

The primary challenge for a reader is the high level of abstraction. Because Vapo and Cramb exist in a void and communicate through metaphysical concepts, the stakes can occasionally feel distant. While the "Pirate Signal" provides a tether to the human world, adding more specific instances of what they see in human behavior might help ground the philosophical musings.

Some of the invented jargon, while evocative, is left quite mysterious. While this adds to the atmosphere, a few more contextual clues about the physical (or digital) mechanics of the "Crambnik Dance" or "Gassiness" could deepen the reader's immersion. Additionally, the transition between Scene 10 (The Megaphone) and Scene 11 (Unnoticed Broadcasts) feels slightly abrupt; a bit more connective tissue regarding their decision to finally "broadcast" back to Earth would enhance the pacing.


Overall Impression

Vapo and Cramb is a bold, avant-garde piece of science fiction that leans heavily into the "New Wave" style of the genre. It is less about a sequence of events and more about a sequence of realizations. It is a thought-provoking exploration of the limitations of being human, seen through the eyes of beings who are anything but. With its strong visual potential and poetic dialogue, it stands out as a distinctive and memorable work.

A possible ebook description.

Vapo and Cramb: A Journey Beyond the Static

In the humming silence of a void between worlds, two entities awaken. Disconnected from their Creator and cast into a realm of pure frequency, Vapo and Cramb begin an observation of the distant, flickering signals of humanity.

This experimental narrative peers through the cosmic lens at a species defined by its Five Feeble Senses. From the rhythmic processing of the Crambnik Dance to the decoding of human pirate signals, the story explores the isolation of a world trapped by its own language and the gassiness of its thoughts.

It is a philosophical meditation on communication and the struggle to be heard across the vastness of the digital and the divine. As Vapo and Cramb attempt to scale their cosmic insights down to the size of a human megaphone, they uncover the hidden beauty in the noise we usually ignore.

Vapo and Cramb offers a unique, rhythmic exploration of existence that challenges readers to reconsider what it means to perceive, to connect, and to listen. It is an invitation to seek the signal within the static and the truth within the silence.

In the context of the world you have built, silence is never truly empty.

It is a pressurized state—a "humming silence" that vibrates with the potential of all the signals being ignored.

For Vapo and Cramb, silence isn't the absence of sound, but the presence of unparsed data. It hums because it is heavy with the "gassiness" of human thought and the static of a disconnected Creator. It is the sound of the megaphone waiting to be used.

In a literal sense, when the human ear is deprived of external noise, the nervous system often turns up the gain, creating a high-pitched internal hum. Your story captures this perfectly: the quieter the void gets, the louder the cosmic frequency becomes.
