But here's what happened with Everything Ebooks: The upload preserved everything. Every intentional fragment stayed. Every choice remained hers. It was waiting for her to start editing.

The Google Docs variation of writing works the same way. Maybe you're a cloud-first writer. Maybe you collaborate with beta readers who are allowed to leave comments. Same door, different handle.

Alex chose this door for the book you're reading now. Started in Google Docs at 3:47 AM in The Night Kitchen—when the house sleeps and doubt is loudest. Downloaded as .docx. (The “I Embrace Simplicity” route)

This door appeals to the minimalists, the plain text advocates, the writers who've been burned by proprietary formats. Your manuscript.txt file is universal. It'll open on any device, in any decade. There's something beautiful about that permanence.

One writer in Rae Kim's community forum put it perfectly: “My novel is 90,000 words. It doesn't need to be anything more complicated than 90,000 words in the right order.”

Chapter 3: The Editing Tools

(My Late-Night Co-Conspirator Has No Pulse)

Alex was back in The Night Kitchen. The manuscript that had felt brilliant six hours earlier now read like something written by a stranger with a personal vendetta. The coffee had gone cold. The confidence, colder. There was a strong temptation to throw the whole thing into Lake Erie.

“This is where most manuscripts die,” he muttered. “Not in the writing. In the space between done and actually done.”

He opened Everything Ebooks with the grim determination of someone about to perform surgery on themselves. This was it. The actual editing. The part where you find out if your baby is beautiful or if you've been lying to yourself for six months.

The interface didn't scream at him. No flashing buttons. No aggressive “BEGIN EDITING NOW” demands. Just a calm little panel that asked, essentially: What do you need help with?

Select a category…

Beneath “AI Tools:” there were two dropdown menus. One said “Select a category.” The other said “Select a tool.” Below them, a few buttons: Prompt, Select, Clear, Run.

Select a tool…

Alex clicked the category dropdown and skimmed the options, he chose Core Editing Tools. The tool dropdown refreshed, he hovered over Narrative Internal Analysis and selected it.

He could have clicked “Prompt” to modify the tool’s instructions, but he was too tired. He clicked “Select” to use the manuscript.txt file.

Alex stared at them. “You're telling me editing my entire manuscript comes down to picking from a menu?”

The dropdown didn't answer. Dropdowns rarely do.

Here's the thing nobody tells you about editing: It's not one thing. It's many things wearing a trench coat pretending to be one thing.

There's the big-picture stuff—does your plot make sense, do your characters grow, does Chapter 7 contradict Chapter 23?

There's the sentence-level stuff—is this clear, is this tight, did you use “very” seventeen times on one page?

There's the nitpicky stuff—is it “lay” or “lie,” do you capitalize Dad when it's used as a name, should you have a comma there or are you just comma-happy at 3:47 AM?

Everything Ebooks doesn't pretend these are the same job. Instead, it gives you a toolbox. You pick the tool you need. You run it. You see what it finds. You decide what to do about it.

After the tool finished running, Alex clicked “View-Edit” and the screen split in two.

On the left: his manuscript. His words. His beautiful, terrible, anxiety-inducing words.

On the right: a report that made Alex's stomach drop and his coffee suddenly taste like regret.

Not because it was cruel. Because it was right.

“Chapter 3, Scene 2: The main character finds the key before she knows it exists. Problem: contradicts discovery scene in Chapter 5. Suggestion: move the key discovery or add a hint.”

Alex scrolled down. More observations appeared like helpful ghosts pointing at the bodies buried in his backyard:

“Weather continuity issue: Chapter 7 opens with a blizzard. Chapter 8, same day, characters are having a picnic. No explanation for weather change.”

“Point of view error: Chapter 12, paragraph 3—Sarah knows about the letter, but she wasn't present when it was read and no one told her about it.”

“Plot logic note: The subplot about the missing painting is introduced in Chapter 4 but never resolved or mentioned after Chapter 9.”

Alex sat back. “Oh. Oh no.”

He'd written this manuscript over six months. He knew every character, every scene, every plot thread. Or he thought he did. Apparently, he'd also created a blizzard that vanished between chapters, a psychic character who knew things she couldn't possibly know, and a painting that disappeared into the Bermuda Triangle of abandoned subplots.

The editing panels waited. No judgment. No mockery. Just a quiet: Well? What are you going to do about it?

Alex clicked into the left panel and started typing fixes. Moved the key discovery to Chapter 5. Added a line about the blizzard clearing by morning. Rewrote Sarah's section from a different character's perspective. Made a note about the painting—either resolve it or cut those early references.

After each change, he clicked Save. The manuscript updated. The tool didn't argue. Didn't insist. Just offered observations and let him decide.

“This is editing,” Alex realized. “This is actual editing. Not someone rewriting my book. Just someone pointing at the holes while I decide how to patch them.”

“Chapter 2: Mysterious locket introduced. Never mentioned again after this chapter.”

Maya groaned. She'd completely forgotten about that locket. It was supposed to be important. It had belonged to the protagonist's grandmother. It was supposed to mean something. Instead, it got introduced and then wandered off into the narrative wilderness, never to be seen again.

“Chapter 9: Cooking contest setup and foreshadowing. No contest scene follows. No resolution provided.”

“Oh, come on,” Maya muttered. But the tool was right. She'd spent three paragraphs setting up this contest, making it seem crucial to the plot, and then... nothing. The contest never happened. Her protagonist's carefully developed cooking skills never got their moment.

“Chapter 5: Foreshadowing about trip home to Puerto Rico. Trip never occurs in manuscript.”

Maya closed her eyes. “I was going to write that scene. I was definitely going to write that scene.”

But she hadn't. And now her manuscript promised readers a homecoming that never came. A Chekhov's gun that never fired. A narrative thread that just sort of... frayed.

The tool wasn't judging her. It was just pointing out what it had found, like a friend saying, “Hey, you left your keys in the door.”

Maya started editing. Added a scene where the locket opened, revealing a photo that explained its significance. Cut the cooking contest setup entirely—if she wasn't going to write the scene, she couldn't promise it to readers. And the trip home? She kept that foreshadowing but added it to her sequel notes. Intentionally unresolved for Book Two.

One thread she left hanging on purpose. One question unanswered. Because some stories don't wrap up every loose end, and that was her choice to make.

The tool offered suggestions. Maya made decisions. That was the partnership.

JORDAN'S LINE EDITING

Jordan approached Everything Ebooks the way a mechanic approaches an engine: methodically, professionally, with reading glasses that wouldn't stay put and a tolerance for tedium that bordered on supernatural.

He selected Core Editing Tools, then Line Editing. Ran it. But Line Editing had a trick the other tools didn't.

When the tool finished, Jordan saw two buttons: View-Edit and One-by-one. He'd used View-Edit before—manuscript on the left, full report on the right. Useful for scanning everything at once. But today he clicked One-by-one.

The interface shifted. Instead of a report to wade through, he got a single issue. Issue 1 of 25.

One-by-one after running Line Editing AI report.

The left panel showed his manuscript with the problem sentence highlighted in the text: "Spud was born in the cellar of the old farmhouse during the most coldest February anyone could remember."

On the right panel, four sections appeared:

ORIGINAL: "Spud was born in the cellar of the old farmhouse during the most coldest February anyone could remember."

ISSUES: "1. Incorrect degree: 'most coldest' doubles the superlative. 2. Clarity/flow: 'during' + 'anyone could remember' works, but tightening increases punch."

SUGGESTED: "Spud was born in the cellar of the old farmhouse during the coldest February anyone could remember."

EXPLANATION: "This keeps your tone and meaning while correcting the grammar and preserving the stark, wintry setup."

At the top, a running tally: Accepted: 0 | Custom: 0 | Pending: 25.

Three buttons waited: Accept, Custom, Skip.

Jordan pushed up his glasses. "Now this is how I want to edit."

He clicked Accept. The manuscript updated.

The tally changed: Accepted: 1 | Custom: 0 | Pending: 24.

He clicked Next. Issue 2 of 25 appeared.

The next one was trickier—a suggestion to tighten a sentence that Jordan thought needed its length for rhythm. He clicked Skip. The tool moved on without argument.

Issue 7 was close but not quite right. Jordan clicked Custom, typed his own revision, and continued.

This was the difference between reading a report and having a conversation. Each suggestion got its moment. Each decision was deliberate. No scanning, no missing things, no "I'll come back to that one later" and then forgetting.

But the real trick? It kept him awake. Every issue demanded a click. Accept. Custom. Skip. No choice meant no progress. Jordan had read manuscripts where his eyes glazed over by page fifty, where he'd realize he'd "read" three paragraphs without absorbing a word. One-by-one made that impossible. The manuscript wouldn't let him nod off. It asked a question and waited for an answer.

The tool flagged sentences that were too long, words that were overused, phrases that could be tighter. But it didn't just correct—it explained why:

"This sentence is 47 words long. Consider breaking into two sentences for clarity?"

"'Very' appears six times on this page. Consider stronger base adjectives?"

"Cliché detected: 'avoid it like the plague.' Consider fresh phrasing?"

Jordan accepted some suggestions, ignored others. A long sentence wasn't always wrong—sometimes rhythm mattered more than rules. "Very" could stay if the character would actually say it. And sometimes a cliché was exactly what the character would reach for.

One by one, issue by issue, the manuscript got cleaner. Not because the tool rewrote it, but because Jordan made twenty-five individual decisions about his words. Accept. Custom. Skip. Each choice his.

The tool made suggestions. Jordan made judgment calls. His glasses slid down his nose. He pushed them back up. The work continued.

THE CUSTOMIZATION SECRET

(The Button You Might Have Missed)

A week later, Maya was in the forum asking about the Copy Editing tool. “It keeps flagging my intentional sentence fragments. I know they're fragments. They're supposed to be fragments. How do I make it stop?”

Rae replied within minutes: “Click ”Prompt” before you run it.”

Maya stared at her screen. “The what now?”

“The Prompt button. In front of Select and Run. You can modify what the tool looks for.”

This was the secret hiding in plain sight. That edit prompt button Alex had seen but been too tired to click? It was the key to making these tools actually yours.

Here's what nobody tells you at first: Each tool isn't some locked, unchangeable process. Each tool is just a prompt—a set of instructions combined with your manuscript. And you can edit those instructions.

Jordan discovered this when he wanted Copy Editing to focus only on grammar, not style suggestions. He clicked “Prompt,” read the tool's default instructions, and added a line: “Focus only on grammar and mechanics. Do not make style suggestions.”

Ran it again. The tool obeyed.

Alex used it when he wanted Narrative Internal Analysis to pay extra attention to a specific subplot. Clicked edit prompt. Added: “Pay special attention to the timeline of the heist subplot in Chapters 8-15.”

The tool did exactly that.

Maya used it to protect her intentional choices. Clicked edit prompt before running Line Editing. Added: “Do not flag intentional sentence fragments used for dramatic effect or character voice. Do not suggest changes to Spanish dialogue or code-switching.”

The tool respected her boundaries.

The workflow became clear:

1. select your Tool

2. if you want to customize it, click edit prompt and modify the instructions

3. click Select to choose your manuscript.txt

4. click Run

5. wait for the Tool to finish

6. click View-Edit to see results

7. make your edits in the left panel

8. click Save

9. if you want to run the same tool again with your changes, just hit Clear and Run again

10. if the results still aren't what you want, click edit prompt again and tweak it

Each tool is just a prompt combined with your manuscript. You can edit the prompt, run the tool, look at results, edit your manuscript, run the same tool again, or try a different one. There's no fixed sequence. No mandatory order. No “you must complete Pass 1 before Pass 2” nonsense.

Rae put it best in her forum post: “The tools aren't a conveyor belt. They're a workshop. You grab the tool you need, use it how you need it, put it back, grab another one. You're the craftsperson. These are just your chisels and sanders.”

Chapter 4: Publishing

(Endeavoring to Persevere)

Nothing to do as the entire process has been an ebook (.epub) all along.

Alex stared at the edited manuscript. Actually edited. Actually done. After seventeen runs through various tools, three existential crises, and that one afternoon using the Opinionated Critique tool just for laughs (verdict: “Your protagonist has the emotional range of a particularly sullen doorknob”), the manuscript was ready.

Now came the part nobody writes books about. The part that feels like doing taxes after painting a masterpiece.

Publishing.

“I hate this part,” Maya said in the web forum at 2 AM. “I spent two years writing this book, six months editing it, and now I have to become a formatting expert?”

Jordan replied within minutes, those perpetually sliding glasses probably still on despite the hour. “You don't have to become anything. That's what the Publishing feature is for.”

Let's be honest: You already know about Amazon KDP. You've watched the YouTube videos. You've read the blog posts. You've probably already uploaded something and discovered that your perfectly formatted Word document looks like it went through a blender.

Publishing is where art meets commerce, and commerce usually wins. It's the place where your beautiful manuscript gets stuffed into boxes labeled “metadata,” “keywords,” and “BISAC codes.”

Publishing in Everything Ebooks doesn't reinvent this wheel—it can't make Amazon less Amazon. What it does is handle the tedious conversion work so you can get back to writing.

THE KDP PUBLISHING PREP

(Mise en Place for Manuscripts)

Alex selected “Other Editing Tools” from the category dropdown, then scrolled to “KDP Publishing Prep.” The tool didn't promise miracles.

It promised to generate the stuff Amazon demands:

- three to five title suggestions (because your working title “Untitled_Final_v2” won't cut it)

- a book description that doesn't sound like you're describing your grocery list

- seven keywords that actual humans might search for

- category recommendations that make sense (well, to Amazon)

- an HTML-formatted description because Amazon likes fancy formatting sometimes

When Alex ran it on this very manuscript, it suggested the title “Everything Ebooks on Everything Ebooks” with the subtitle “A Writer's Guide to AI-Assisted Editing.” Not brilliant. Not terrible. Functional—like everything about publishing.

The description it generated hit all the Amazon requirements: no promotional language, no “Hey, please review this!” desperate pleas. It was... fine. Which is all you need for the algorithm gods.

Here's what the Publish feature won't do:

- Make publishing fun, just faster

Here's what it will do:

- Get your formatted files ready without making you cry

- Generate the tedious metadata everyone requires

- Create clean files that actually work

- Save you hours of formatting frustration

THE PHILOSOPHY OF GOOD ENOUGH

Rae Kim posted something in the forum that stuck with everyone: “Publishing isn't writing. Publishing is what happens so people can read your writing. Don't let perfect formatting be the enemy of published books.”

She was right. The Publishing feature isn't trying to be revolutionary—it's trying to be functional. Your book description doesn't need to win a Pulitzer. It needs to tell readers what your book is about. Your keywords don't need to be poetry. They need to help readers find your book.

Maya's first novel went live on KDP six months after she discovered Everything Ebooks. The formatting was clean. The description was clear. The keywords worked. It wasn't a bestseller, but readers found it, bought it, read it, and some even reviewed it.

“I spent two years writing it and fifteen minutes formatting it for publication,” she said. “That seems about right.”

When Alex created this book for publication, the process was embarrassingly simple:

1. Run KDP Publishing Prep for the metadata

2. Create as EPUB for e-readers

3. Create as PDF for print preview

4. Upload to KDP, both separately

5. Wait for Amazon to process, twice

6. Panic that something went wrong, twice

7. Realize nothing went wrong, twice

8. Publish

The whole process took an hour, including the panic break.

Publishing is a necessary evil. It's the bridge between your manuscript and readers. The Publish tool doesn't try to make it fun or revolutionary. It just attempts to make it less painful.

Your manuscript is edited. Your files are formatted. Your metadata is ready. You're ready for the part everyone hates but everyone does anyway: sending your words into the world.

Jordan summed it up best: “I charge thousands to edit manuscripts. I charge nothing to format them for publishing because it's boring and I'd rather be editing. Let the robots handle the boring parts.”

So let them.

Your real work—the writing, the editing, the agonizing over every word—is done. Publish is just the delivery truck that gets your book to readers. Unglamorous but essential. Like spell check, or coffee, or reliable Wi-Fi.

Time to publish. Time to panic. Time to move on to the next book.

Because that's what writers do. We write. Publishing is just what happens between books.

Now go upload your book before you talk yourself out of it. Let the Publish feature handle the formatting. You handle the courage part.
